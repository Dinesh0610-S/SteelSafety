# Indian Factories Act 1948: Hazardous Processes & Personnel Safety

## SECTION 41-F: Permissible Limits of Exposure
In heavy industries handling hazardous processes (such as steel plants and coke oven batteries), the occupier must maintain work environments within permissible occupational exposure limits (OEL).
For Carbon Monoxide (CO), the 8-hour Time Weighted Average (TWA) is 25 ppm, and the short-term exposure ceiling is 100 ppm. Any environment exceeding 100 ppm presents severe risk of carbon monoxide poisoning.
For Hydrogen Sulfide (H2S), the exposure ceiling is 10 ppm. Any concentration above 20 ppm is immediately dangerous to life and health (IDLH).

## SECTION 36: Precautions in Confined Spaces
No person shall be allowed to enter any chamber, tank, vat, flue, pipe, or other confined space in any factory in which gas, fume, or dust is likely to be present to such an extent as to involve risk to persons, unless:
1. A certificate in writing has been given by a competent person based on a test that the space is free from dangerous gas.
2. The worker wears suitable breathing apparatus and a safety belt securely attached to a rope, the free end of which is held by a person outside.
Exceeding exposure limits inside a confined space without a certified permit represents a direct statutory violation.

## SECTION 87: Dangerous Operations and Personnel Clustered Hazards
Where any operation carried on in a factory exposes persons employed to serious risk of bodily injury, poisoning, or disease, the state government may make rules restricting the number of workers.
Clustering of personnel (defined as 4 or more workers present in the same hazard zone) dramatically amplifies the consequence severity of any gas release, converting localized exposures into mass-casualty events.
If gas sensors exceed high warnings (CO >= 100 ppm or H2S >= 10 ppm), immediate evacuation and headcount must be conducted for all personnel clustered in the zone.
